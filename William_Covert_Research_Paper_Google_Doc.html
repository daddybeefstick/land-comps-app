The Modern-Day Matrix

Generative AI has moved from a specialized technology to an everyday tool in colleges. Recent student surveys have shown that AI use is very widespread and commonly used for assignments (Freeman). This shift matters because college research and projects have a purpose: to create skills such as evaluating sources, critical thinking, and effective reasoning, rather than just producing a final product. At the same time, research shows a pattern: AI can improve the quality of students' work, but it often leads students to become reliant on it and has a negative effect in the long run (Wang and Fan; Risko and Gilbert; Gerlich). This paper investigates how AI usage and overreliance affect research, assignment quality, knowledge retention through cognitive offloading, and how college policies and rubrics shape how and why students use AI. The central question is whether AI acts as a substitute for learning or a teacher that guides students and helps them.

This paper looks at three areas: how using AI as support versus replacement affects research and work quality, how cognitive offloading changes what students actually retain from assignments, and how different college policies and assignment rubrics can change how or if AI is used, and therefore its impacts.

Background and Context

Throughout time, new ways of teaching and learning have always been a core concept of education. From using papyrus to write books to modern technology, education is always changing, and so are the ways people teach. Recently, advances in technology, such as AI, have caused a massive stir in the educational community, as both students and teachers have begun to see just how powerful and futuristic AI can be, as long as it is utilized correctly. AI has been pushed and developed exponentially, and since it first became popular, changes in open-source AIs, such as ChatGPT, have been drastic. How people approach AI has changed drastically as well in the last few years. With the current trend of usage, people will most likely rely on AI or become too dependent on it and miss the point of AI. A Pew Research Center study reports that ChatGPT use for schoolwork among teens doubled from 2023 to 2025, so the trend is only accelerating (Pew Research Center).

As AI tools have become more developed, they have gone from just being able to help with grammar to being able to write doctoral-level papers and sound like experts in fields that people have spent years studying. Because of the massive changes, people (mainly students) have realized this and decided to use AI to complete their schoolwork, and instead of just using it as a helpful tool, actually have it do the assignments in full for them. In college, that shift matters because research and projects are supposed to help students learn new skills and gain new knowledge, not just complete them and forget about them. In college, students have to do a lot of grueling but necessary work—such as finding credible sources, taking notes, contrasting people's views, and then taking all that work and making it into a clear presentation. AI can help with this process, but it should not be doing the work for them. For example, students sometimes use AI as a research assistant, where they can use it to summarize work and help create outlines, but the main part is that they still have to read and verify the work and do the projects themselves. When AI is used like that, it becomes a great tool that helps and teaches students, as a teacher would, and should be implemented in colleges.

However, the problems start when students stop using AI as support and start using it as a replacement. If a student uses AI to do their work, sure, they can create something that looks great, makes sense, and seems thorough, but none of that matters because if the student took shortcuts, then they learned nothing. That is where the problem lies for many colleges and universities, because the line between AI being a helpful tool and being a replacement is blurred and can flip in an instant. On top of that, students sometimes use AI for group projects, which can jeopardize students who did not use AI and potentially ruin their college scholarships, while the students affected had no idea AI was even used.

Research Quality and Accuracy

When students use AI as a support tool for planning, revising, or making guiding questions, it can improve the quality of research writing without destroying how they learn. AI is not some niche issue that schools face anymore. One UK survey found that 92% of students have used AI in some way, and 88% have used generative AI to help with assessments (Freeman). AI has been built into many modern students' routines and workflows, even on assignments they do not need it for. A meta-analysis of 51 studies found that ChatGPT can improve students' learning outcomes when AI is used properly instead of being used to do the work for them (Wang and Fan). The analysis reported large positive effects on learning performance (g = 0.867) and moderate effects on learning perception and higher-order thinking. The main point here is that AI can raise the quality of work for students because it can make it easier for them to do schoolwork and correct problems that might have slipped past them previously. What matters for research quality is not the presence of AI but whether students remain in the loop—reading, verifying, and deciding. However, there are limitations that are definitely still needed to prevent students from just using AI to do their work, skim over it, and then turn it in, even if it looks like they understand the topic.

Despite the benefits of AI, the quality of work can degrade fast, even if students are using it to help, especially when they stop double-checking AI's information. The University of Washington's library highlights hallucinations, which is when AI makes something up, such as fake evidence (University of Washington Libraries). This becomes a major problem, especially when AI is used for research purposes that will be shared with the public or professionals who do not have time to verify a paper's sources. The Harvard Kennedy School Misinformation Review also explained that the reason AI is so effective at spreading unintended misinformation is that it generates it to fit the problem or question at hand, and it sounds so good and believable that students do not check it (Shao). The more polished the AI output looks, the less incentive students have to verify it—and that is exactly when verification matters most. For a college researcher, the habit of verification is the skill; skipping it for convenience trades short-term ease for long-term weakness. AI is not just a classroom issue either; it is becoming a problem with false sources everywhere, in work and in day-to-day life, and if limits do not get placed on it soon by schools and educational institutions, then finding real, credible sources will be even harder. Overall, AI can improve a student's work drastically if utilized correctly. However, once a student becomes too trusting of AI, their quality of work can plummet, and their research can fall apart.

Cognitive Offloading and Knowledge Retention

When people stop using AI as a support tool and use it as the means to complete an assignment, students might finish their work faster and even seem smarter, but they usually retain less knowledge and fewer skills than the teacher wanted. The goal of college is not just to produce projects that have a result; they are to teach skills and habits that stick, not just used for one assignment.

Recently, a term has become popular with researchers in this field called cognitive offloading, which is basically giving the brain's job to an outside tool like AI (Risko and Gilbert). Offloading is not necessarily bad on its own, but when offloading becomes a common thing that students do constantly, it hurts their thinking and leads to negative effects (Gerlich). A randomized controlled trial published in 2025 tested this. Undergraduates were supposed to learn their material with ChatGPT as a study aid or with traditional methods. On a surprise retention test 45 days later, the ChatGPT group had a much lower score of knowledge retained at 57.5% versus 68.5% retained (Barcaui). The researchers concluded that overreliance on ChatGPT was likely to reduce the cognitive effort that builds better memory (Barcaui). In other words, AI can help learning, but sometimes helping is not what students need when they are trying to learn.

These same ideas come up with critical thinking. A 2025 mixed study with 666 participants found that people scored less on critical thinking skills when they relied on AI more often than when they did not, specifically linking it to cognitive offloading (Gerlich). They concluded that when people rely on AI a lot, they cannot make their own decisions because AI makes them for them (Gerlich). The point of this study is not that AI makes someone intellectually incompetent; it is that a heavy reliance on AI can train students to skip making tough decisions that further their learning abilities and judgment skills. Those same skills that get diminished are the same skills that the assignments are trying to build.

Overall, people who use AI as a replacement often end up in a weird place: higher-looking products, but lower actual knowledge. They can turn in work and claim it is their own, but when someone has them actually try to explain what they turned in at a deep level, they cannot. That disconnect matters for colleges because the goal is not just to produce papers but to produce thinkers who can defend their work. That is why the issue at hand is not just AI usage; it is trying to find an intricate balance of where using AI is good, but where students can find the Goldilocks zone of how much and how to use it. Even if the final product looks good, the long-term results from just blatantly using AI without actually learning the content are severe, especially when students are not forced to explain it to someone or do the work themselves (Risko and Gilbert).

Institutional Policy and Assignment Design

College policies matter more than ever now; unclear rules lead to confusion and misuse of AI in educational settings. UNESCO argues for the proper use of AI, mainly through teaching AI literacy and how to use AI as a tool, and not as something that will disappear from education (Miao and Holmes). The EDUCAUSE AI Landscape Study goes into detail about how colleges and universities are still developing their rules and regulations on AI, and how they have to change just as quickly as AI evolves (EDUCAUSE). The study reports that 73% of institutions cite rising student use as a top reason for planning, and 68% cite risks of inappropriate use. When colleges set clear goals, they find that students are more likely to utilize AI properly, instead of just using it to complete work. However, when the guides are vague, AI becomes more of a replacement because students would rather get the work done than spend the time to do it properly.

Even with strong guidelines, AI can still be used incorrectly, especially when it is used to find resources and do research. A major survey on hallucination talks about how AI often goes and makes up sources to prove itself correct, which creates problems for students who use AI for college and citations (Ji et al.). Generated summaries can still sound accurate while small parts or details are added or cut out, meaning students end up using false information that is not actually supported by their source (Maynez et al.). This matters a lot for college research because students have to be able to present credible evidence and back it up, especially in higher-level degrees. Chan and Colloton, in Generative AI in Higher Education: The ChatGPT Effect, emphasize that course design has to account for AI by structuring assignments that require students to show their reasoning, submit drafts, and explain how evidence was selected (Chan and Colloton). If AI is used during research, then stricter rules need to be in place to prevent false sources or summaries from being put into actual research that has real impacts.

Grading and assignment rubrics matter because students usually try to focus most heavily on whatever earns them the most points. TEQSA talks about how, since AI can generate writing and essays quickly, colleges need to figure out ways to adapt to this and make assignments that still promote real learning and tasks that force students to prove that they are knowledgeable in their subject (TEQSA). The QAA talks about something similar; they talk about how courses and colleges should change and adapt new standards so AI can be treated as normal, but not as something that can do all the essential work (Quality Assurance Agency). When assignments have to have drafts that are edited by people, or notes, or other ways to engage students, it helps to blend in with implementing AI while preventing it from doing everything.

Counterperspectives and Limitations

Not all evidence and studies point to the fact that AI is harmful to learning. Several studies have found and argue that generative AI can help improve learning outcomes when it is used in assignments that require verification, revision, and reflection through papers and projects, rather than results. In those scenarios, the question should not be if AI was used at all, but whether it improves the students' ability to read sources, select evidence, and explain their reasoning and conclusions. Crompton and Burke identify affordances like 24/7 support, writing assistance, and help explaining difficult concepts, alongside challenges including inaccuracies, hallucinations, and bias (Crompton and Burke). Likewise, Zhang et al. show that outcomes vary by how students use the tool, not just whether they use it, and that students who understand AI better tend to use it more responsibly (Zhang et al.). These findings reinforce that the distinction between support and replacement is what matters, not AI presence alone.

Research on AI and student learning is not perfect or even close to being done, so it is hard to make a single claim that fits how AI affects work quality and retention of information. A lot of studies rely on students reporting their own use of AI, which seems great on paper until students lie or just do not report it at all. Studies also sometimes measure the quality of work using basic features, such as organization, clarity, and grammar, even though actual research quality should be about source accuracy and thoroughness, correct citations, and combining all the ideas into one coherent response. Another limitation is that findings can change quickly because AI is changing so fast, which makes results very situational. Finally, retention studies do not always accurately reflect the students' knowledge because factors such as assignment types and what learning strategies were used matter. These limitations suggest that AI effects are more situational than universal (Risko and Gilbert; Barcaui).

Compromise and Potential Solutions

A good compromise is not just banning AI or letting students use it for everything. Colleges need clear rules that explain what AI is allowed to do as a support tool and what counts as replacing students' work. For example, schools can allow AI for brainstorming, outlining, grammar help, or making guiding questions, but restrict it from writing paragraphs, making citations, or creating sources that students just copy and paste into the projects (Chan and Colloton; Quality Assurance Agency). That kind of policy making is a great compromise, and it matters because it removes the gray area and makes the rules clear, and does not block out students so they do not have or feel the need to cheat.

The biggest fix, though, is changing how assignments are graded. If the only thing that is graded is the final result, then students have no reason not to let AI do all the work, because it will most likely be more well rounded than their work. TEQSA explains that assignments should be designed so students use AI responsibly and ethically in ways that match the rubric, but also help them (TEQSA). That supports assignments that force students to show their work, have checkpoints with drafts, and give short explanations of how and why evidence was chosen for the sources used. It does not ban AI, but it makes it a lot harder for students to cheat, as they actively have to explain their work themselves.

A compromise also has to address the issue of knowledge retention, not just the quality of work in the final product. Research on cognitive offloading shows that students learn and remember more when they practice using their memory in assignments and produce work on their own, and when they do work that requires mental strain instead of taking shortcuts (Risko and Gilbert; Barcaui). In topics that usually have a lot of AI usage, that could mean adding parts to assignments like note checks after drafts, or proof of their own work, and a reflection that states whether AI was used or not, and for what. With rules like these, AI becomes more like a support tool than a replacement, all while forcing students to actually learn and retain the information.

Conclusion

Overall, the evidence shows that AI tools do change how college students approach research and project-based work, but the outcomes depend mainly on how the AI is used, not if it is used at all. When students use AI for support for planning, revising, or getting ideas and sources, the final products are often improved without destroying the learning process (Wang and Fan). However, when students let AI replace the majority of work and research, such as looking at sources, choosing evidence, and making decisions, it leads to cognitive offloading, which lowers retention of knowledge and defeats the purpose of the assignments (Risko and Gilbert; Gerlich; Barcaui). This answers the research question in a way that leaves it open to the two main answers out there: AI can either strengthen or weaken research habits depending on whether it is used as a guide or a shortcut. The main issue for colleges is not getting rid of AI, but setting clear rules and guidelines that have steps to engage students and prove their work and thought processes with evidence. What complicates things is that the same assignment can invite either kind of use—a paper that rewards only the final product creates an incentive to substitute, while one that requires drafts and evidence of process creates an incentive to support. Students will focus on whatever gets graded. Using those guidelines, AI is more likely to support a better quality of work while keeping long-term retention of knowledge and skill development, as colleges intend.
