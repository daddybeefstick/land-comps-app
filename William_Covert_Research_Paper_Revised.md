The Modern-Day Matrix

Generative artificial intelligence has shifted from a specialized technology to a routine academic tool in higher education. Surveys indicate that the majority of college students now use AI in some form for coursework, with generative AI increasingly integrated into research and project-based assignments (Freeman). This shift matters because college research and projects are designed to develop skills such as source evaluation, critical thinking, and reasoned argumentation—not merely to produce finished products. At the same time, research suggests a pattern: AI can improve the quality of work produced, yet it can also lead students to become reliant on external tools in ways that undermine long-term learning (Wang and Fan; Risko and Gilbert; Gerlich). This paper investigates how AI usage—specifically the distinction between AI as a support tool and AI as a replacement for student thinking—affects research accuracy, assignment quality, knowledge retention through cognitive effort and offloading, and how institutional policies and assignment design shape student behavior. The central inquiry is whether AI functions as a scaffold for learning or as a substitute that short-circuits the development of research and analytical skills.

The paper investigates the following: how the support-versus-replacement distinction affects research quality and source accuracy; how cognitive offloading influences knowledge retention; and how college policies and assignment design can encourage productive rather than substitutive AI use.

**Background and Context**

Open-source generative AI tools such as ChatGPT have developed rapidly since late 2022, expanding from basic assistance to systems capable of producing advanced academic-style text. This expansion has coincided with a sharp increase in student adoption. A 2025 survey of United Kingdom higher education students found that 92% had used AI in some form and 88% had used generative AI to help with assessments (Freeman). The survey, published by the Higher Education Policy Institute, draws from a large student sample and reflects current norms of AI use in academic settings. A separate Pew Research Center study reports that use of ChatGPT for schoolwork among American teens doubled from 2023 to 2025, indicating that AI integration into academic work is accelerating across age groups (Pew Research Center). College research and project-based work are intended to build skills such as identifying credible sources, synthesizing conflicting viewpoints, and presenting evidence clearly. When students use AI as a research assistant for brainstorming, outlining, or summarizing, they can retain control over the intellectual work. When they rely on AI to generate full paragraphs, produce citations, or make analytical decisions, the line between support and replacement blurs. Defining that line—and understanding its consequences for research quality and knowledge retention—is the focus of this investigation.

**AI as Support: Potential for Improved Research Quality**

When students use AI as a support tool for planning, revising, or generating guiding questions, it can improve the quality of research writing without undermining learning. A meta-analysis of 51 studies published between November 2022 and February 2025 found that ChatGPT can positively affect students’ learning performance, learning perception, and higher-order thinking when AI is used appropriately rather than to complete work for students (Wang and Fan). The analysis reported large positive effect sizes on learning performance (g = 0.867) and moderate positive effects on learning perception and higher-order thinking. The significance of these findings is that outcomes depend on how AI is used: when students employ it to scaffold their own reasoning—for example, to test ideas, refine arguments, or check clarity—the tool can raise the quality of work while preserving the cognitive effort required for learning. The 92% and 88% figures from the Freeman survey indicate that AI is no longer marginal but embedded in student workflows. That level of use suggests both widespread acceptance and potential dependency; students may increasingly assume AI assistance is expected, which raises questions about how institutions and instructors distinguish acceptable support from unacceptable replacement. Wang and Fan’s meta-analysis does not simply show that AI helps; it indicates that the benefit is conditional on integration that preserves student agency. The main implication is that AI can raise work quality when it corrects overlooked errors and streamlines labor-intensive tasks, but only if limits exist to prevent students from submitting AI-generated work with minimal engagement (Wang and Fan). Clear institutional definitions of support versus replacement are therefore essential.

**When Support Becomes Replacement: Quality Degradation and Misinformation**

When students stop verifying AI output and begin treating it as a substitute for their own research and analysis, work quality can deteriorate quickly. The University of Washington Libraries defines “hallucinations” as instances in which AI fabricates references, claims, or evidence that do not exist or are unsupported by sources (University of Washington Libraries). Hallucinations are not rare edge cases; they occur when models generate plausible-sounding text to satisfy a prompt without grounding it in accurate data. Shao argues that AI is particularly effective at spreading unintended misinformation because it produces content tailored to the question at hand, and the output often sounds convincing enough that users do not verify it (Shao). For college students conducting research, this creates a specific risk: they may incorporate fabricated citations, misattributed statistics, or distorted summaries into papers, believing the AI output is reliable. The quality degrades not because the prose is poor, but because the underlying evidence is false or unverifiable. When students rely on AI for source discovery, summarization, or citation generation without checking against original sources, their ability to judge credibility and conduct independent research weakens. The problem is primarily a classroom and academic one: if students habitually accept AI output without verification, they fail to build the judgment skills that research assignments are designed to develop. Over time, this undermines their capacity to evaluate sources, assess evidence, and produce research that meets scholarly standards.

**Cognitive Offloading and Knowledge Retention**

When students use AI as a replacement rather than a support tool, they may complete assignments faster and produce work that appears competent, but they retain less knowledge and fewer skills than intended. Risko and Gilbert define cognitive offloading as the transfer of cognitive work to external tools; offloading itself is not inherently harmful, but it changes what learners store, practice, and internalize (Risko and Gilbert). When offloading becomes habitual, students perform fewer of the mental operations that build durable memory. Gerlich’s 2025 study of 666 participants found strong negative correlations between AI tool use and critical thinking (r = −0.68) and strong positive correlations between AI tool use and cognitive offloading (r = +0.72); offloading and critical thinking were also strongly negatively correlated (r = −0.75) (Gerlich). The implication is that heavy reliance on AI can train students to outsource decision-making and analysis, which in turn weakens the skills that research-based assignments aim to develop.

A randomized controlled trial by Barcaui tested knowledge retention among undergraduates who learned material either with ChatGPT as a study aid or with traditional methods. On a surprise retention test 45 days later, the ChatGPT group scored 57.5% correct compared with 68.5% for the traditional group (Barcaui). The researchers concluded that overreliance on ChatGPT likely reduced the cognitive effort required to build durable memory. In other words, AI can support learning when it augments student effort, but when it replaces that effort, retention suffers. Gerlich’s findings extend this to critical thinking: when students depend on AI for decisions and analysis, they engage less frequently in the demanding reasoning that strengthens judgment. The assignments that use AI are often the same ones intended to build those skills; if AI completes the work, the opportunity for learning is lost. Students may submit polished products while lacking the ability to explain their reasoning or defend their evidence.

**Institutional Policy and Assignment Design**

College policies and assignment structures significantly influence whether students use AI as support or replacement. Unclear or absent guidelines create ambiguity that encourages substitutive use. UNESCO argues for treating AI as a permanent feature of education, to be governed through AI literacy and explicit rules rather than ignored or banned (Miao and Holmes). The 2024 EDUCAUSE AI Landscape Study reports that 73% of institutions cite rising student use of AI as a top reason for strategic planning, and 68% cite risks of inappropriate use (EDUCAUSE). The study also notes that policies are evolving rapidly as institutions try to keep pace with AI development. When colleges articulate clear boundaries—what AI may and may not be used for—students are more likely to use it as a support tool. When guidance is vague, students tend to prioritize completing work quickly over engaging deeply.

Assignment design matters as much as written policy. TEQSA argues that because AI can generate essays and reports quickly, colleges must redesign assessments so that students demonstrate genuine learning rather than only submit final products (TEQSA). The Quality Assurance Agency similarly recommends that institutions adapt standards and teaching approaches so that AI is integrated as a tool while essential cognitive work remains with students (Quality Assurance Agency). Chan and Colloton, in *Generative AI in Higher Education: The ChatGPT Effect*, emphasize that course design must account for AI’s presence by structuring assignments that require students to show their reasoning, submit drafts, and explain how evidence was selected (Chan and Colloton). When rubrics reward only the final submission, students have little incentive to use AI as support rather than replacement; when assignments include checkpoints, drafts, and reflections, substitutive use becomes harder to sustain. Ji et al. and Maynez et al. document that AI frequently fabricates sources and introduces factual errors into summaries that read fluently (Ji et al.; Maynez et al.). For research-heavy assignments, stricter verification expectations—such as requiring students to confirm citations against original sources—can reduce the risk that hallucinated content enters graded work.

**Counterperspectives and Limitations**

Not all evidence suggests that AI harms learning. Several studies indicate that generative AI can improve outcomes when used in assignments that require verification, revision, and reflection. Crompton and Burke identify affordances such as 24/7 support, writing assistance, personalized feedback, and help explaining difficult concepts, alongside challenges including inaccuracies, hallucinations, and bias (Crompton and Burke). Zhang et al. distinguish between ChatGPT used to support learning and ChatGPT used to displace learning effort, finding that outcomes vary with how students use the tool (Zhang et al.). These findings suggest that the question is not whether AI was used, but whether it improved students’ ability to read sources, select evidence, and explain reasoning. Outcomes depend on integration and purpose.

Research on AI and student learning has limitations. Many studies rely on self-reported AI use, which can be underreported or inaccurate. Some assessments emphasize surface features such as organization and grammar rather than source accuracy, citation correctness, and analytical depth. AI evolves quickly, so findings may become outdated. Retention studies vary by assignment type and learning strategy, and not all measure long-term memory. These limitations suggest that conclusions about AI’s effects are more situational than universal.

**Compromise and Potential Solutions**

A workable approach is neither blanket prohibition nor unrestricted use. Colleges need clear rules that specify what counts as support versus replacement. Support might include brainstorming, outlining, grammar assistance, and generating guiding questions; replacement might include AI-written paragraphs, AI-generated citations, or AI-produced sources that students submit without verification (Chan and Colloton; Quality Assurance Agency). Such policies reduce ambiguity and give students explicit boundaries.

The most consequential change is redesigning how assignments are graded. If only the final product is assessed, students have little reason to avoid letting AI complete the work. TEQSA recommends designing assessments so that students use AI responsibly and in ways aligned with learning objectives (TEQSA). That supports requirements such as drafts, checkpoints, and short explanations of how and why evidence was chosen. Assignments that demand proof of process—notes, drafts, reflections on AI use—make substitutive use harder while allowing support.

Research on cognitive offloading and retention indicates that students learn and remember more when they practice retrieval and produce work themselves (Risko and Gilbert; Barcaui). Incorporating elements such as note checks, draft reviews, and reflections on whether and how AI was used can reinforce learning while accommodating AI as a support tool. With clear policies and redesigned assignments, AI is more likely to function as support than as replacement.

**Conclusion**

The evidence indicates that AI tools do change how college students approach research and project-based work, and that outcomes depend primarily on how AI is used rather than whether it is used at all. When students use AI for planning, revising, or generating ideas while maintaining control over research and analysis, work quality can improve without undermining learning (Wang and Fan). When students allow AI to replace research, source selection, and analytical decision-making, cognitive offloading reduces knowledge retention and weakens the skills that assignments are meant to build (Risko and Gilbert; Gerlich; Barcaui). The central distinction is between AI as a scaffold for student thinking and AI as a substitute for it. The main challenge for colleges is not to eliminate AI but to establish clear guidelines and assignment designs that encourage supportive use while requiring students to demonstrate their reasoning and verify their evidence. Future decisions would benefit from prioritizing explicit definitions of support versus replacement, assessment reform that values process as well as product, and ongoing adjustment as AI and research continue to evolve.
