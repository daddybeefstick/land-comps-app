RESEARCH POSTER TITLE
AI in Student Research: Support vs. Replacement
Place below title
THESIS (1-2 LINES)
When generative AI is used as a shortcut that replaces reading, note-taking, and drafting, college students' research quality and knowledge retention tend to decline; when it is used as a support tool with verification and reflection, it can strengthen the writing process without replacing critical thinking.
WHY THIS TOPIC MATTERS — 5" × 2.25"
  • AI tools are now common in student research and writing (Freeman).
  • Main risk: AI hallucinations—false details and facts (UW Libraries; Shao; Ji et al.).
  • AI can help with outlining, revising, explanations if students fact-check.
  • Affects learning, knowledge retention, and skill development.
PATHWAY 1: AI IN STUDENT WORKFLOW — 5" × 4.25"
  • Many students use AI for brainstorming, summarizing, quick explanations (Freeman).
  • Speeds up planning but can replace close reading if students copy output instead of checking sources.
  • AI changes workflow when it replaces steps (reading, notes) instead of guiding.
  • Best outcomes: AI like a teacher, not someone who does the work.
PATHWAY 2: QUALITY OF WORK — 5" × 4.50"
  • AI improves surface-level quality (clarity, grammar, organization) for feedback/revision (Wang and Fan).
  • Heavy reliance reduces deeper thinking and originality (Gerlich).
  • AI can make work look good but should not replace building arguments or evaluating evidence.
  • Best outcomes: AI used like a teacher, not a replacement.
PATHWAY 3: RETENTION / LEARNING — 5" × 4.00"
  • When AI gives final answers, students retain less—skip practice (Barcaui).
  • Higher AI reliance linked to lower critical thinking (Gerlich).
  • AI helps if used for practice questions, self-explanations, study planning.
  • Retention depends on whether AI reduces thinking or increases active learning.
PATHWAY 4: MODERATING FACTORS — 5" × 3.00"
  • Clear policies and AI literacy matter; vague rules encourage misuse (UNESCO).
  • Assessment design matters—if only final product is graded, students skip real thinking (TEQSA).
  • Assignment structure matters more than the tool itself.

LIMITATIONS — 5" × 3.50"
  • Many studies rely on self-reported AI use or short-term outcomes; long-term effects understudied (Gerlich).
  • AI changes fast—findings can become outdated as models and rules evolve (EDUCAUSE).
OVERALL CONCLUSION / WHAT I LEARNED — 5" × 3.00"
  • Outcomes depend more on how AI is used than whether it is used (Wang and Fan; Risko and Gilbert).
  • Best solution: clear rules and assignments that require evidence of understanding, drafts, explanations (TEQSA).
  • Biggest risk: better-looking work with less retention and critical thinking.
